K-means

Illustration of the algorithm

$$ \begin{split} &\text{Step1: Randomly initialize K centroids, }\mu_1,\mu_2,…,\mu_k\\space\ &\text{Step2: Assign points to closest centroids, }c^{(i)}.\\space\ &\text{Step3: Recompute the locations of centroids}\ &\quad\quad\quad\text{by calculating the average of the points assigned to them.}\\space\ &\text{step4: repeat Step2 and Step3, until there is no further change.} \end{split} $$

NOTICE

  1. In step1, the most commonly used way is to randomly choose K training examples(points) and set them as the centroids.

  2. You need run K-means multiple times(usually 50 to 1000 times) to avoid local minima(not global minima).

Cost function

$$ \begin{split} &J={1\over m}\sum\limits_{i=1}^m||x^{(i)}-\mu_{c^(i)}||^2\ &m:\text{the number of training examples.}\ &x^{(i)}:\text{a single training example.}\ &c^{(i)}: \text{the index centroid that i th point assigned to.}\ &\mu_i: \text{the i th centroid.}\ &||x^{(i)}-\mu_{c^{(i)}}||: \text{the distance between the i th point and}\ &\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\space\text{the centroid it assigned to.} \end{split} $$

Anomaly detection

Gaussian(Normal) distribution

$$ \begin{split} &p(x)={1\over\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}e^{-(x-\mu)^2\over 2\sigma^2}\ &\text{x is a number.}\ &\mu\text{ is the mean of all data.}\ &\sigma\text{ is the standard deviation and }\sigma^2\text{ is variance.} \end{split} $$

Density estimation

$$ \begin{split} &p(\overrightarrow x)=\prod\limits_{i=1}^n p(x_i;\mu_i,\sigma_i^2)\ &\overrightarrow x\text{is a vector(training example) with n features.}\ &\mu_i,\space\sigma_i^2\text{are corresponding mean and variance.} \end{split} $$

Algorithm description

$$ \begin{split} &\text{Step1: Choose n features that are indicative of anomalous examples.}\ &\text{Step2: fit parameters.}\ &\mu_j={1\over m}\sum\limits^m_{i=1}x_j^{(i)}\quad\quad\text{(vectorized)} \overrightarrow\mu={1\over m}\sum\limits^m_{i=1}{x^{(i)}}\ &\sigma_j^2={1\over m}\sum\limits^m_{i=1}(x_j^{(i)}-\mu_j)^2\quad\quad\text{(vectorized)} \overrightarrow{\sigma^2}= {1\over m}\sum\limits^m_{i=1}(x^{(i)}-\overrightarrow\mu)^2\ &\text{Step3: Given a new example x, calculate p(x) using formula in }\ &\quad\quad\quad\text{density estimation.}\ &\text{Step4: Compare p(x) with }\epsilon\text{ that you choose.} \text{ If }p(x)<\epsilon,\ &\quad\quad\quad\text{the example can be anomalous otherwise it is OK.} \end{split} $$

NOTICE

  1. the feature must follow Gaussian distribution. If it doesn’t, you can make some change(like using log(x)) to make it more Gaussian.

  2. Compared with supervised learning, this algorithm is more suitable where it is hard to learn the feature of anomalous data from existent dataset.
